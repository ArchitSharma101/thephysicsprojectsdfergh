Waves Collide, Patterns Emerge

By Archit Sharma, Dev Bharwada, Mrityunjay Manas, Ayush Jha, Vishal Dumre, Areeb Syed
Introduction :
Diraction is the bending or spreading of waves as they encounter obstacles or pass through
openings. In the context of optics, it refers to the modication of light patterns as it interacts with
objects or openings, creating distinctive patterns of varying intensity.

Conditions for Diraction :
Coherent light source is crucial for well-dened diraction patterns.
The size of the obstacle or opening should be comparable to the wavelength of the waves.
Diraction Gratings :
Arrays of closely spaced slits used to disperse light into components based on wavelength.
Applications :
Used in optical instruments such as microscopes, telescopes, and laser systems.
Plays a crucial role in understanding the wave nature of light.
Diraction Limit :
Denes the limit to the resolution of optical instruments due to the wave nature of light.
Conclusion :
Diraction phenomena provide valuable insights into the behavior of waves, contributing to
advancements in optics and our understanding of the nature of light.

SINGLE SLIT DIFFRACTION
Introduction:
In the single-slit diraction experiment,
we can observe the bending phenomenon of light or diraction that causes light from a
coherent source to interfere with itself and produce a distinctive pattern on the screen
called the diraction pattern.
Diraction is evident when the sources are small enough that they are relatively the
size of the wavelength of light.
For large slits, the spreading out is small and generally unnoticeable.

Double slit diraction:
Basic arrangement and working:
Youngs double slit experiment uses two coherent sources of light placed at a
small distance apart which interfere with each other and produce diraction
patterns. Usually, only a few orders of magnitude greater than the wavelength of
light are used. Youngs double slit experiment helped in understanding the wave
theory of light.
In the double slit experiment, we use two long parallel slits as the sources of
light in place of pin holes. The light coming out of the two slits is intercepted on
a screen placed parallel to the plane of the slits. The slits are illuminated by a
parallel beam of monochromatic light.
